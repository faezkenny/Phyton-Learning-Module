ABSTRACT

This study presents a comparative analysis of various fore-

casting models, including ARIMA, ETS, and Prophet, used

on product sales data from a Walmart stor e. Using the Root

Mean Square Error as our main performance metric, we

identify the strengths and weaknesses of each model when

it comes to making predictions on time series data.

1 INTRODUCTION

In a rapidly changing and moving society full of data, it is

important for businesses to be able to make informed and

timed decisions. This is crucial for the success of a business.

Various forecasting methods have been proven to be an ef-

fective strategy when applied to this world full of data for

strategic planning and operational eciency. For supermar-

ket stores, such as Walmart, forecasting sales data can be

a useful strategy to plan what products to buy and store

in order to gain the most prot. By accurately predicting

sales trends, supermarkets can ensure that they maintain

sucient stock levels to meet customer demand without

overstocking, which can lead to waste and increased holding

costs. Besides this, forecasting may also give insights into

customer behaviour [

1

], gives possibilities and insights into

risks and challenges [

2

] and can help businesses optimize

their resources [4].

There are numerous forecasting methods to choose from,

from basic methods as naive forecasting and moving aver-

ages, to more complex methods, such as ARIMA and even

machine learning methods. For most of these methods, it is

important to understand certain patterns in the data when

to choose which model for the best results [

3

]. However,

for a task like sales forecasting for hundreds of products, it

is not eective to visualize each plot and humanly decide

which method the data ts the best. This project focuses on

methods that do not need visualizations or parameter tuning

for all instances to choose a model that ts the data the best.

Instead, it looks at deciding which method would work the

best based on performance on the training set.

A method that is used frequently that applies to this is

Prophet [

6

], developed by Meta/Facebook. Prophet is a fore-

casting tool designed to handle time series data with daily

observations that display patterns on dierent time scales,

such as holidays and weekly seasonality. Prophet requires

minimal parameter tuning compared to some complex mod-

els and it can automatically detect and incorporate seasonal

patterns into the forecasting process.

Additionally, this method can incorporate regressors [

5

].

Regressors are external variables that can be incorporated

into the forecasting model to capture the impact of additional

factors on the time series data. These external variables could

be considered as dynamic regression components [

3

] because

they introduce a dynamic relationship between the predictor

variables and the target variable over time. Regressors are

often used to account for the inuence of external factors

that may aect the time series being forecasted. These could

include economic indicators, marketing campaigns, or any

other relevant variables that are not part of the intrinsic time

series components like trends, seasonality, and holidays.

In short, this paper aims to identify techniques and meth-

ods to eectively forecast multiple instances without much

human parameter tuning. This paper will compare Prophet

to other forecasting methods for tasks that are complex. This

gives us the following research questions:

(1)

How does the performance of various forecasting

models compare in predicting sales data with diverse

patterns, including trends, seasonality, and random

uctuations?

(2)

Is Prophet an eective method to forecast sales data,

with a lot of products?

W e think that a combination of models that suit the data

best for each instance may be useful, as it allows for multiple

dierent kinds of seasonality and trends for each instance.

An ensemble method might be useful. Secondly, we think

that Prophet may be an useful method, as it states that it auto-

matically detects seasonality and trends in the data without

having to address these manually.

has been used:

Data preprocessing

Before forecasting methods can be applied to the data, the

use of some data preprocessing techniques was necessary.

Since forecasting is usually done with time-series data [

3

],

we ensured that each dataset was transformed into a tsib-

ble. Additionally, transforming the data to a tsibble makes

it possible to work with the data in an R-environment. The

'date' column of the data has been transformed from a 

to 

 in this process and was set as the index. After-

wards, 'event-types' were one-hot encoded, which created

four dierent columns that included a binary representation

of the presence of event types. This is a way to deal with the

NaN-values, and thus makes it easier to feed this information

to dierent types of models.

The price dataset contained dates with weeks. Since both

the calendar and the price dataset contained 'wm-yr-wk'-

values, a merge was done with these values as key. A left-join

was performed, which created a combined dataset with daily

calendar data for all products, including the daily price (based

on weeknumber) when a price was present (not-NaN). This

is convenient, as it already removes NaN-values for products

that are not in stock. Based on visualizations for for example

product 2, it was clear that some products are not always in

stock. When applying forecasting methods, we do not have

to worry about NaN-values for products, which can be seen

in gure 2 for product 2, where the data starts after a while.

Thirdly, the sales dataset was prepr ocessed. The dataset

has been transformed in such a way that it was able to be

merged with the other datasets. For this, we added 'date' as

a separate column, instead of a lot of columns of dates. The

row names for the date (previously column names) were then

transformed into their respective date. Since the days were

ordered and there were no NaN-values, it was possible to

change the values to the starting date (2011-01-29) and add

an extra day for each next row. This data was then able to be

merged with the combined dataset on the common columns

'product' and 'date'. A left-join was done, which ensured

that the sales data was aligned to the combined dataset, and

thus the combined dataset stayed the same in length. This

resulted in a combined 'tsibble' (1,246,617 x 10 [1D]) with

each 'product' as key [823].

2 FORECASTING METHODS

The following forecasting methods have been used. Methods

1 to 4 have been used in combination with each other, where

the last 28 days of the training set have been used to nd the

most suitable model. Methods 5 to 7 have been trained and

tested for comparisons.

(1) Mean Model

Description:

The Mean model is one of the sim-

plest forecasting methods. It predicts future val-

ues by calculating the average (mean) of the past

data. This model assumes that the best predictor

of what will happen tomorrow is the average

of what has happened up till today. It is most

eective when the data does not show strong

trends or seasonal patterns.

Justication:

The Mean model is selected for its

simplicity and eectiveness in situations where

the data is relatively stable and lacks signicant

trends or seasonality. Its simplicity makes it a

good benchmark for comparing more complex

models.

(2) Seasonal Naive Model

Description:

The Seasonal Naive model is a vari-

ation of the Naive forecasting method. It makes

predictions based on the last observed value

from the same season in the previous cycle. For

example, in monthly data with yearly seasonal-

ity, it uses the value from the same month in the

previous year.

Justication:

This model is chosen because some

products exhibit strong seasonal patterns. It is

particularly useful when seasonality is the domi-

nant feature of the data,which is subject to change

per analyzed product.

(3) Random Walk Model

Description:

In the Random Walk model, the pre-

diction for the next period is simply the value

observed in the last period. It assumes that the

best predictor for tomorrow is today's value.

Justication:

The Random Walk model is selected

because it performs better when the data shows

a strong level of random variability and no clear

trend or seasonal patterns.

(4) Drift Model

Description:

The Drift model extends the Ran-

dom Walk model by allowing the predictions to

change over time. It does this by incorporating

a trend (drift) calculated from the historical data.

The forecast is a linear function of the drift and

the last observed value.

Justication:

The Drift model is useful when the

time series data shows a consistent trend over

time. It's chosen for data where the change from

one period to the next is relatively constant, mak-

ing it suitable for products with a linear trend

over time.

(5) ARIMA Model

Description:

ARIMA (AutoRegressive Integrated

Moving Av erage) is a popular statistical method

for time series forecasting. It combines autore-

gressive features (relying on past values) with

moving average components and dierencing to

make the data stationary (removing trends and

seasonality).

Justication:

ARIMA is selected for its exibility

and eectiveness in handling a variety of time

series patterns, including data with trends and

seasonality.

(6) ETS Model

Description:

ETS (Error, T rend, Seasonality) is a

comprehensive model that accounts for error,

trend, and seasonality in the data. It uses expo-

nential smoothing to give more weight to recent

observations and is capable of modelling com-

plex seasonal patterns.

Justication:

The ETS model is chosen because

some products exhibit clear trends and seasonal-

ity. Its ability to model both components makes

it a robust choice for a wide range of seasonal

time series data, particularly when the pattern

of seasonality and trend is consistent over time.

(7) Prophet

Prophet is a forecasting model de-

3 RESULTS

Methodology

In this part of our project, due to computational limitations,

we were unable to construct a combined or ensemble model.

Instead, we focused on comparing the accuracies of various

individual forecasting models to understand their capabili-

ties in predicting complex sales data. The models employed

include ARIMA, ETS (Error, T rend, Seasonality), Random

Walk, Drift, Mean, and Seasonal Naive. Each of these models

was chosen for its unique approach to handling time series

data, oering a diverse perspective on forecasting accuracy.

The eectiveness of these models was evaluated using a

training-validation split. This approach involved running all

the benchmarked 'simpler' models on all available products,

thus enabling us to assess their performance in a real-world

context. The Root Mean Square Error (RMSE) was calculated

for each model and then averaged across all products to pro-

vide a comprehensive measure of accuracy. The resulting

RMSE scores for these models, which reect their prociency

This methodology allowed us to draw insights into the strengths

and limitations of each model when applied to sales forecast-

ing. By analyzing a range of models, we aimed to ascertain

which models are most adept at handling the complexities

inherent in sales data, such as seasonal variations, trends,

ARIMA:. is particularly eective for our data set due to its

comprehensive approach to modelling time series. It inte-

grates three key components: autoregression (AR), dier-

encing (I), and moving average (MA). The AR component

leverages the dependency between an observation and a

number of lagged observations. The I component helps in

making the time series stationary, a crucial step for many

time-series models, by dierencing the data a certain number

of times. Finally, the MA component models the error term

as a linear combination of error terms at various time lags.

This blend allows ARIMA to handle a wide range of time

series data, especially non-stationary data, which is common

in sales forecasting.

ETS:. this model usually excels in scenarios where data ex-

hibits clear trend and seasonal patterns. ETS models decom-

pose a time series into error, trend, and seasonal components,

modeling each separately. This approach is particularly bene-

cial for sales data, which often demonstrates clear seasonal

trends (e.g., higher sales during certain holidays or seasons)

and underlying trends (such as increasing or decreasing sales

over time). The ETS model's ability to capture these inher-

ent patterns in the sales data likely contributed to its strong

performance.

Random Walk and Dri: models are simpler time series mod-

els. A Random Walk model assumes that the future value is

simply the last observed value plus a random step. The Drift

model extends this by allowing for a consistent upward or

downward trend (drift) over time. While these models are

capable of capturing some level of trend in the data, their

simplicity limits their ability to deal with more complex pat-

terns, especially when it comes to handling seasonality and

non-linear trends. This explains their moderate performance

compared to the more sophisticated ARIMA and ETS models.

Seasonal Naive: is a straightforward yet contextually rele-

vant approach for time series forecasting, especially when

the data exhibits clear seasonal patterns. This model assumes

that the value of a series at a particular season in the future

will be equal to the value observed at the same season in

the past. For instance, in a monthly sales dataset with strong

seasonal trends, the forecast for July of a given year would

be the actual sales value from July of the previous year. In

our analysis, the Seasonal Naive model displayed a higher

average RMSE compared to more sophisticated models like

ARIMA and ETS, and it also had a the lowest count of being

the best model. This outcome suggests that while the model

is capable of capturing seasonality, it might not be as eec-

tive in datasets where other factors, such as trends 'cyclic

patterns,and randomness play a signicant role.

Mean: The Mean model's performance in our analysis was

unexpectedly impressive, as highlighted by its high count of

being the best model for forecasting. This model, which pre-

dicts future values by averaging past observations, demon-

strated a notable eectiveness, reected in both its third-

lowest RMSE score and its highest frequency of being cho-

sen as the best model. This dual achievement was somewhat

surprising, considering the model's simplicity.

The Mean model's strength lies in its ability to smooth

out short-term uctuations, thereby emphasizing long-term

trends. In scenarios where data uctuations are relatively

random and not strongly correlated with specic trends or

seasonal patterns, the Mean model's simplistic approach of

averaging can eectively lter out noise, providing a stable

forecast. This is particularly useful in datasets with high vari-

ability or when the data does not follow predictable patterns.

The fact that the Mean model not only scored well in terms

of RMSE but also emerged as the most frequently chosen

model suggests a signicant aspect of our dataset. It indi-

cates that a considerable proportion of the products may

have sales data that do not conform to strong, predictable

seasonal or trend-based patterns. In such cases, the simplic-

ity of the Mean model becomes its greatest asset, allowing

it to avoid the potential pitfalls of overtting or misinter-

preting complex patterns, common challenges with more

sophisticated models.

Prophet model's ecacy in sales data forecasting. Developed

by Facebook, the Prophet model distinguishes itself through

its adept handling of seasonality, holiday eects, and non-

linear trends, which are prevalent in sales data scenarios.

Shortcomings:

Prophet operates on an additive model framework that al-

lows for separate yet cohesive modelling of dierent time

series components. This approach is particularly eective

in handling complex seasonal patterns and irregular trends,

Adjusting the Prophet Model :

in our study, we strategically employ ed the additive model

framework, a cornerstone feature of the Prophet model, to

enhance its forecasting capabilities. W e made the decision of

using an additive method instead of multiplicative for com-

the sales over time, while the seasonality component cap-

and yearly uctuations.

The holiday component, although limited by computational

ular, event-driv en spikes in sales. This additive structure

not only facilitated a more comprehensive understanding of

the underlying patterns in the data but also provided ex-

ibility in adjusting the model to accommodate the unique

characteristics of the sales dataset.

Comparison with ARIMA and ETS Models:

While ARIMA and ETS are procient in capturing linear

relationships and simple seasonal patterns, our analysis in-

dicates that they are less eective in scenarios with com-

plex seasonality and irregular trends – areas where Prophet

demonstrates its strengths. The exibility of ARIMA in model

specication and the ETS model's components (error, trend,

and seasonality) make them suitable for certain datasets but

not as universally applicable as Prophet in the context of

sales forecasting.

Simplicity vs. Complexity in Forecasting Models:

Our study also considers the Drift and Random Walk models,

known for their simplicity and ease of understanding. De-

spite their utility in certain situations, these models were gen-

erally outperformed by the Prophet,ETS,and ARIMA models

indicating a trade-o between simplicity and accuracy in

forecasting models.

Mean Model :

The performance of the Mean model within our analysis

presents an interesting case for discussion. Despite its fun-

damental approach, the model not only achieved the third-

lowest RMSE score but also emerged as the preferred forecast-

ing model for approximately 30 per cent of the products. This

dual accomplishment is particularly noteworthy given the

simplicity of the model. The core strength of the Mean model

lies in its capacity to average historical data, which eec-

tively smooths out short-term anomalies while accentuating

long-term trends. This attribute becomes especially valuable

in datasets where uctuations are more random and less

tied to discernible trends or seasonal patterns. In such con-

texts, the Mean model's straightforward averaging approach

serves to eciently lter out noise, thus yielding a more sta-

ble forecast. This becomes an asset in scenarios characterized

by high data variability or an absence of predictable patterns.

The notable performance of the Mean model, both in terms

of RMSE and its frequent selection as the optimal model, sug-

gests a distinctive characteristic of our dataset: a signicant

5 CONCLUSIONS

In conclusion, our study oered a comprehensive analysis of

various forecasting models, each with its distinct advantages

and shortcomings. The ARIMA and ETS models, known for

their ability to capture linear relationships and simple sea-

sonal patterns, demonstrated superior performance in terms

of RMSE scores. However, their eectiveness decreased in

scenarios with complex seasonality and irregular trends, ar-

eas where the Prophet model showed its strengths. The Mean

model, despite its simplicity, was surprisingly eective.

The Prophet model was employed for all products to cap-

ture seasonality, tr ends, holidays, and regressors. While this

model theoretically allows for a signicant impact of holi-

days on sales forecasting, computational limitations hindered

our ability to fully integrate and assess these eects.

A major limitation in our study was the inability to test the

simpler models (ARIMA, ETS, Random W alk, Drift, Mean) on

the test set. This gap in our methodology restricts the conclu-

siveness of our results. The simpler models, such as Random

Walk and Drift, wer e capable of capturing some level of trend

in the data, but their simplicity limited their ability to handle

more complex patterns, particularly in terms of seasonality

and non-linear trends.

The Mean model, on the other hand, stood out for its ability

to smooth out short-term uctuations, emphasizing long-

term trends. Its simplicity became its greatest asset, allowing

it to avoid pitfalls like overtting or misinterpreting complex

patterns.

Overall, our research indicates a trade-o between simplic-

ity and accuracy in forecasting models. While the Prophet

model's parameters may not have been optimized due to

computational issues, its additive seasonality was chosen

based on constant variance over time. However, this decision,

along with the possible oversights in capturing correlations

between products and the full impact of holidays, points to

potential areas for improvement in forecasting accuracy.

Future work

To further increase the accuracy of the time series pr edic-

tions, we propose ideas for future work. Firstly, having more

powerful computational resources would facilitate a more

in-depth analysis of the Prophet model, allowing us to com-

prehensively evaluate the best hyperparameters for more

accurate forecasting. Additionally, conducting an ensemble

modelling approach by combining the strengths of multiple

forecasting models could oer a more robust and accurate

prediction framework, especially given the presence of di-

verse and complex sales patterns. Furthermore, by merging

our data with other datasets, we could consider external fac-

tors, such as economic factors or a broader scope of events,

that may be correlated with sales numbers. These could be

used as additional features in forecasting models and could

potentially contribute to a better understanding of the dy-

namics of store sales and lead to better predictions.
